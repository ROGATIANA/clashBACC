ENGLISH AUXILIARY 🇬🇧 CHAPTER 1
Understanding auxiliary verbs: be, do, have, and modal auxiliaries
🇬🇧

English Auxiliary Verbs

Primary auxiliaries (be, do, have) · Modal auxiliaries (can, could, may, might, must, shall, should, will, would)

Complete lesson

📚 INTRODUCTION

Every sentence must have a verb.

  • To depict doable activities, writers use action verbs.
  • To describe conditions, writers choose linking verbs.

Be, do, and have are both stand-alone verbs and auxiliary verbs. When these verbs are auxiliary, you will find them teamed with other verbs to complete the verb phrase.

📌 Key Definition:
An auxiliary verb (or helping verb) is a verb that adds functional or grammatical meaning to the clause in which it appears. It helps the main verb express tense, mood, or voice.

🔤 PRIMARY AUXILIARIES

BE (am, is, are, was, were, being, been)

Used to form:
- Continuous tenses: I am reading
- Passive voice: The book was written

DO (do, does, did)

Used to form:
- Negative sentences: I don't know
- Questions: Do you like coffee?
- Emphasis: I do want to go

HAVE (have, has, had, having)

Used to form:
- Perfect tenses: I have finished
- Perfect continuous: She has been working

⚠️ MODAL AUXILIARIES

📌 Modal auxiliaries are:
can, could, may, might, must, shall, should, will, would
They express possibility, necessity, permission, ability, obligation, or prediction.

CAN / COULD

Can: ability, permission, possibility
I can swim. (ability)
Could: past ability, polite request, possibility
I could run fast when I was young.

MAY / MIGHT

May: possibility, permission (formal)
May I come in?
Might: less certain possibility
It might rain later.

MUST / HAVE TO

Must: strong obligation (internal)
I must finish my work.
Must not: prohibition
You must not smoke here.

SHALL / SHOULD

Shall: suggestion, future (formal, with I/we)
Shall we dance?
Should: advice, recommendation
You should see a doctor.

WILL / WOULD

Will: future, intention, promise
I will call you tomorrow.
Would: past habit, polite request, conditional
He would always help us.

📝 COMMON VERB PHRASES

📌 Examples of auxiliary + main verb combinations:

- am/is/are + -ing → Present continuous: She is reading
- was/were + -ing → Past continuous: They were playing
- have/has + past participle → Present perfect: I have seen
- had + past participle → Past perfect: She had left
- will + base form → Simple future: We will arrive
- be + past participle → Passive voice: The cake was eaten

❓ NEGATIVE AND QUESTION FORMS

Negative Forms

Auxiliary + not
Examples:
- I do not (don't) know
- She is not (isn't) coming
- They have not (haven't) finished
- He cannot (can't) swim

Question Forms

Auxiliary + subject + main verb
Examples:
- Do you like coffee?
- Is she coming?
- Have they finished?
- Can he swim?

✅ SHORT ANSWERS

📌 Structure of short answers:
- Affirmative: Yes, subject + auxiliary
- Negative: No, subject + auxiliary + not

Examples:
- Do you speak English? → Yes, I do. / No, I don't.
- Has she arrived? → Yes, she has. / No, she hasn't.
- Will they come? → Yes, they will. / No, they won't.

💡 SUMMARY & KEY POINTS

📌 Key Takeaways:
- Primary auxiliaries: be, do, have (used to form tenses, voice, questions, negatives)
- Modal auxiliaries: can, could, may, might, must, shall, should, will, would
- Modals express possibility, necessity, permission, ability, obligation, prediction
- Auxiliaries are always followed by a main verb (except in short answers)
- In negative sentences: auxiliary + not
- In questions: auxiliary + subject
- Short answers repeat the auxiliary verb only
