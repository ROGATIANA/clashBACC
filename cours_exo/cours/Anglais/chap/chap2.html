Question Tags

Formation rules · Auxiliary verbs · Special cases · Intonation patterns

Complete lesson

❓ INTRODUCTION

Question tags are short questions at the end of statements.

They are mainly used in speech when we want to:

  • Confirm that something is true or not
  • To encourage a reply from the person we are speaking to

Question tags are formed with the auxiliary or modal verb from the statement and the appropriate subject.

📌 Basic Structure:
Statement + comma + auxiliary/modal + subject + question mark

📏 BASIC RULES

Rule 1

We use a positive question tag with a negative sentence

Ex: She isn't coming, is she?

Rule 2

We use a negative question tag with a positive sentence

Ex: You are coming, aren't you?

Rule 3

We use contracted form = not (n't) always in question tag

Ex: She likes coffee, doesn't she?

Rule 4

In the end we use question mark (?)

Ex: It's a beautiful day, isn't it?

🔧 WITH AUXILIARY & MODAL VERBS

Common auxiliary and modal verbs: TO BE, TO HAVE, TO DO, WILL, CAN, MUST, WOULD, SHOULD, COULD

When you see one of these, it's easy to answer — just use the same auxiliary/modal in the tag!

📌 Examples:
- She is a doctor, isn't she?
- They have finished, haven't they?
- You will help me, won't you?
- He can swim, can't he?
- We must go now, mustn't we?

⚡ WITHOUT AUXILIARY VERBS

When there is no auxiliary verb in the statement, we use do, does, or did in the question tag.

Present Simple (without he/she/it)

Use DO / DON'T

You like fish, don't you?

Present Simple (with he/she/it)

Use DOES / DOESN'T

He likes fish, doesn't he?

Past Simple

Use DID / DIDN'T

She liked fish, didn't she?

⚠️ SPECIAL CASES & EXCEPTIONS

📌 Negative words:
Nobody, Nothing, Never are negative statements, so the question tag should be positive.
Nobody called, did they?
Nothing is wrong, is it?
📌 Exceptions:

1. "I am"
I am attractive, aren't I?
(Note: "am I not" is grammatically possible but "aren't I" is the natural form in English)

2. "Will" → "won't"
You will pass the BAC, won't you?

3. "Let's"
Let's go to the beach, shall we?

🎵 INTONATION

📌 Falling intonation (when we are sure of the answer):
When we are sure of the answer and we are simply encouraging a response, the intonation in the question tag goes down.
This is your car, isn't it?
(Your voice goes down when you say "isn't it.")
📌 Rising intonation (when we are not sure):
When we are not sure and want to check information, the intonation in the question tag goes up.
He is from France, isn't he?
(Your voice goes up when you say "isn't he.")

💡 SUMMARY & KEY POINTS

📌 Key Takeaways:
- Question tags turn statements into questions for confirmation or encouragement
- Positive statement → Negative tag
- Negative statement → Positive tag
- Use the same auxiliary/modal if present in the statement
- Use do/does/did if no auxiliary is present
- Nobody, nothing, never are negative → positive tag
- Falling intonation = sure of the answer
- Rising intonation = not sure, checking information
