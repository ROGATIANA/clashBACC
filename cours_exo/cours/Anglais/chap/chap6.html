Causative form: have + object + past participle · get + object + past participle
🇬🇧

Causative Form

Have something done · Get something done · Differences · Uses · Examples

Complete lesson

📚 INTRODUCTION

Have something done and get something done are both used to refer to actions which are done for the subject rather than by the subject.

Causative verbs are used instead of passive verbs to show that the subject causes the action to be done.

📌 Basic Structure:
Subject + have/get + object + past participle

🔧 HAVE SOMETHING DONE

📌 Structure:
have + object + past participle

Example:
I don't know how to repair cars, so I'm having mine repaired at the garage round the corner.

🔧 GET SOMETHING DONE

📌 Structure:
get + object + past participle

Examples:
I really must get my eyes tested. I'm sure I need glasses.
Get your hair cut!

📊 KEY DIFFERENCES

Have Something Done

✓ More formal
✓ Used in formal writing
✓ Neutral tone
I had my car repaired.

Get Something Done

✓ More informal / colloquial
✓ Used in everyday speech
✓ More frequent in imperative form
Get your hair cut!

📌 Note:
The differences between "have" and "get" something done are that:
- Have is slightly more formal than "get"
- Get is more frequent than "have" in the imperative form

💡 USES OF THE CAUSATIVE FORM

📌 We use the expression "to have something done" (have + object + past participle) to say:

1. That somebody does something for you. Usually they're things that are difficult to do yourself or you don't want to do yourself.
I had my house painted last summer.
She had her hair cut at the salon.

2. That something unpleasant happened to somebody.
He had his wallet stolen on the subway.
They had their roof damaged in the storm.

⏰ CAUSATIVE FORM IN ALL TENSES

Tense Structure Example
Present Simple have/get + object + past participle I have my car serviced every year.
Present Continuous am/is/are having/getting + object + pp I am having my hair cut now.
Past Simple had/got + object + past participle She had her dress made.
Past Continuous was/were having/getting + object + pp I was having my car repaired.
Present Perfect have/has had/got + object + pp He has had his eyes tested.
Future (will) will have/get + object + past participle I will have my house painted.
Imperative get + object + past participle Get your hair cut!

❓ QUESTIONS AND NEGATIONS

📌 With "have":
Questions and negations of the verb "have" are formed with do/does or did.

Do you have your car serviced regularly?
I don't have my clothes ironed.
Did she have her hair cut?
📌 With "get":
Did you get your car fixed?
I didn't get my nails done.

🗣️ INFORMAL USAGE

📌 Note:
We can use the verb "get" instead of the verb "have" only in informal conversation.

Formal: I had my teeth checked.
Informal: I got my teeth checked.

📝 COMMON EXPRESSIONS WITH CAUSATIVE FORM

get/have your hair cut

faire couper les cheveux

get/have your car repaired

faire réparer la voiture

get/have your eyes tested

faire contrôler la vue

get/have your house painted

faire peindre la maison

get/have your photo taken

se faire photographier

get/have something stolen

se faire voler quelque chose

💡 SUMMARY & KEY POINTS

📌 Key Takeaways:
- Causative form = someone does something FOR the subject
- Structure: have/get + object + past participle
- Have something done = more formal
- Get something done = more informal (more common in imperative)
- Used for: services (hair cut, car repair) and unfortunate events (wallet stolen)
- With "have": questions/negations use do/does/did
- "Get" is only used in informal conversation
